What documents do you need for a business loan? The complete checklist

By Felstow Bookkeeping & Consulting · Updated July 2026

Every lender phrases it the same way — "just send over your financials" — and every borrower discovers it means a lot more than one QuickBooks export. Here is the full list, what the loan officer is checking in each document, and how to hand it over so the answer is yes.

The core package every lender asks for

  • Profit & loss statement (12–24 months, by month). The single most-read document. The officer looks for revenue trend, margins, and whether the business is consistently profitable — not just profitable in total.
  • Balance sheet. What the business owns and owes as of today. They check working capital, existing debt, and whether owner draws are draining the company.
  • Business tax returns (2–3 years). Used to verify the P&L. Large gaps between the two are the fastest way to lose credibility.
  • Business bank statements (3–12 months). Proof the cash flow on paper shows up in the account: average balances, deposit patterns, NSF incidents.
  • Debt schedule. Every existing loan, lease, and credit line with lender, balance, rate, and monthly payment. This feeds the ratio that decides most applications — debt-service coverage.
  • Personal financial statement & personal tax returns. For most small-business loans the owner guarantees the debt, so personal assets, liabilities, and income are underwritten too.

What larger or SBA loans add

  • Cash flow projections (12–24 months forward), with the assumptions written down.
  • Accounts receivable / payable aging — how fast customers pay you and you pay vendors.
  • Business plan or use-of-funds statement — one page explaining exactly what the money buys and how it produces the cash to repay itself.
  • SBA forms — for a 7(a) application, expect SBA Form 1919 (borrower information) and a personal financial statement (historically Form 413), plus articles of organization, licenses, and your lease. See the full SBA 7(a) requirements guide.

What the loan officer actually does with the stack

They are not reading for pleasure. Underwriting reduces your documents to a handful of numbers: the debt-service coverage ratio (can monthly cash flow carry the new payment with room to spare), revenue trend, net margin, liquidity, and the consistency between your P&L, tax returns, and bank statements. If those numbers are easy to find, you look organized and fundable. If the officer has to assemble them from raw exports, you look risky — and risky-looking files get declined.

How to present the package

  1. Reconcile first. Un-reconciled books produce numbers that shift between documents. Match your P&L to your bank statements before exporting anything.
  2. Send PDFs, not spreadsheets. A spreadsheet invites editing questions; a clean PDF reads as a finished statement. Here is the full preparation walkthrough.
  3. Lead with a summary page. One page with revenue, net income, existing debt service, and your projected DSCR including the new loan. You have answered the underwriter's first question before they ask it.
  4. Label everything. "Hargrove-Landscaping-PL-Jul25-Jun26.pdf" beats "export (3).csv" — small signal, real effect.

Frequently asked questions

How many months of financials do lenders want?

Most lenders want a 12-month profit & loss at minimum, and many ask for 24 months to see a full seasonal cycle plus year-over-year comparison. Bank statements typically cover the last 3–12 months.

Do I need a CPA to prepare the documents?

Usually not for small-business loans. Lenders routinely accept borrower-prepared statements generated from QuickBooks or similar software, as long as they reconcile against tax returns and bank statements. CPA-reviewed or audited statements are generally only requested for larger commercial credits.

What is a debt schedule?

A one-page table listing every existing business debt — lender, original amount, current balance, interest rate, monthly payment, and maturity date. Lenders use it to compute your total debt service and your debt-service coverage ratio.

Can I apply with less than two years in business?

Yes, but expect more scrutiny. With a short history, lenders lean harder on bank statements, projections, your personal credit, and collateral. Some SBA programs are specifically friendlier to younger businesses.
