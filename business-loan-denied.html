Why business loans get denied — 9 reasons and how to fix each one

By Felstow Bookkeeping & Consulting · Updated July 2026

Lenders rarely tell you the real reason beyond a form letter. But decline reasons are boringly predictable — underwriting is a checklist, and applications fail at the same nine points over and over. Here they are, each with its fix.

1. Debt-service coverage below the bar

The number-one substantive decline. If projected cash flow doesn't cover all debt payments — including the new loan — by roughly 1.25×, most banks pass. Fix: lengthen the term, shrink the ask, consolidate an existing high-payment debt into the new loan, or wait for a stronger trailing twelve months. Run the ratio yourself before any lender does.

2. Books that don't reconcile

P&L says one thing, tax return says another, bank statements say a third. Underwriters read inconsistency as risk, whatever the cause. Fix: reconcile every account, then prepare one coherent package where every number traces.

3. Undocumented add-backs

Depreciation, amortization, and one-time expenses legitimately boost your cash-flow figure — but only when claimed and documented. Borrowers who submit raw net income routinely understate their own coverage by 15–30%. Fix: list each add-back with evidence.

4. The ask is wrong-sized

Too large for the cash flow and it fails the ratio; suspiciously round with no use-of-funds story and it fails the smell test. Fix: derive the number from something — a quote, a purchase agreement, a working-capital calculation — and show the derivation.

5. Thin or bumpy revenue history

Under two years of history, or wild month-to-month swings, makes projected cash flow feel unreliable. Fix: present monthly (not just annual) figures with the seasonality explained in writing; consider SBA programs, which are structurally friendlier to younger businesses.

6. Personal credit problems

On a guaranteed loan, the owner's credit is the business's credit. Fix: pull your reports before applying, fix errors, pay down revolving balances, and attach a short written explanation for any legitimate past event — underwriters respond well to candor with dates.

7. Existing debt load

Stacked MCA advances or maxed credit lines can push total debt service past what any new lender will join. Fix: make the new loan a consolidation that demonstrably lowers total monthly payments — that's a strengthening move, not a weakness.

8. Industry or concentration risk

Some lenders simply don't do restaurants, construction, or trucking; others balk when one customer is half your revenue. Fix: you can't change your industry, but you can pick lenders who lend in it (ask before applying) and address concentration head-on with contract terms or customer history.

9. A messy, incomplete file

The quiet killer. Missing documents, unlabeled exports, numbers the officer has to assemble by hand — files like this get set aside, and set-aside files become declines. Fix: submit the complete checklist, organized, labeled, with a one-page summary on top.

Reapplying after a denial

A decline is data, not a verdict. Ask the lender which factor drove it (they'll usually tell you when asked directly), fix that factor, and reapply — at that lender in 3–6 months or immediately at one whose credit box fits you. Borrowers who return with a visibly stronger, better-organized file convert at rates that would surprise you; the file itself is the signal that things changed.

Frequently asked questions

Does a business loan denial hurt my credit?

The denial itself is not reported. The lender's hard credit inquiry (if they made one) has a small, temporary effect. There is no penalty flag that follows the business around — you can reapply elsewhere immediately.

How long should I wait before reapplying?

At the same lender, wait until the factor they cited has genuinely changed — often 3–6 months. At a different lender with a different credit box, you can apply right away; just fix the diagnosable problems first so you're not collecting identical declines.

Will lenders tell me why I was denied?

Adverse-action rules require lenders to state the principal reasons for a denial, and most will elaborate if you ask the loan officer directly and non-defensively. That conversation is free underwriting advice — always have it.
