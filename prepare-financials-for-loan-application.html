How to prepare financial statements for a loan application

By Felstow Bookkeeping & Consulting · Updated July 2026

A CPA will build your loan package for $500–$1,500 and a two-week turnaround. For most small-business applications you can produce the same thing yourself in an afternoon — if you know what loan officers scan for. This is the walkthrough.

Step 1: Reconcile before you export

Every account — checking, savings, credit cards, loans — reconciled to its statement through the most recent complete month. This is non-negotiable: an underwriter's first verification is your P&L against your bank statements, and un-reconciled books fail it. In QuickBooks Online: Settings → Reconcile, work each account to a zero difference.

Step 2: Clean the five things officers notice

  1. Miscategorized owner spending. Personal expenses in business categories overstate costs (hurting your ratio) and undermine trust when spotted. Reclassify to draws/distributions.
  2. "Uncategorized" and "Ask My Accountant" balances. These lines scream unfinished books. Zero them out.
  3. Negative expense months or weird spikes. Usually a refund or a misposted journal entry — find it and fix or footnote it, because the officer will ask.
  4. Loan payments booked as expenses. Principal belongs on the balance sheet, only interest on the P&L. Getting this wrong misstates income in both directions.
  5. Revenue timing games. If December is mysteriously double, be ready to explain it. Consistency matters more than any single month.

Step 3: Export the right reports

  • Profit & Loss, last 12–24 months, Display columns by: Months — the monthly view is what shows trend and seasonality.
  • Balance Sheet as of the most recent month-end.
  • A/R and A/P aging summaries if requested.

Step 4: Build the analysis layer

This is the part borrowers skip and CPAs charge for: the summary that turns statements into an argument. Lenders want to see your debt-service coverage ratio with the proposed loan included, revenue trend, margins, and cash position — computed, charted, and stated in plain English, with add-backs (depreciation, amortization, one-time costs) explicitly claimed.

Step 5: Package and label

  • One PDF per document, clearly named: BusinessName-PL-Jul25-Jun26.pdf.
  • A one-page cover summary: what you're requesting, what it's for, the key numbers (revenue, net income, DSCR with the new loan), and a sentence on trend.
  • The full checklist in one email or portal upload — complete files move through underwriting; drip-fed ones stall.

When you genuinely do need a CPA

Three cases: the lender explicitly requires reviewed or audited statements (common only on larger commercial credits), your books have structural problems you can't untangle (years un-reconciled, inventory chaos, cash/accrual confusion), or the loan is large enough that professional polish materially affects pricing. For a routine small-business term loan or line of credit, borrower-prepared statements from clean QuickBooks data are accepted every day.

Frequently asked questions

Do lenders accept QuickBooks-generated financial statements?

Yes — borrower-prepared statements from QuickBooks or similar software are standard for small-business lending, provided they reconcile with your tax returns and bank statements. Reviewed or audited statements are typically only required for larger commercial loans.

Cash or accrual basis for a loan application?

Match your tax returns — consistency is what underwriters check. If you keep books on accrual but file on cash, export the P&L on the same basis as the returns, or provide both with a note.

What if my last 12 months include a bad stretch?

Don't hide it — explain it. A short written note ("Q1 dip: lost anchor client in January, replaced by March; see recovery in monthly figures") converts a red flag into evidence of resilience. Officers decline what they can't explain to committee, not necessarily what dipped.
